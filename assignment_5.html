Introduction

The Pacific giant salamander, Dicamptodon tenebrosus, are large salamanders to the Pacific Northwest of the North America. These salamanders inhabit around bodies of water such as creeks, ponds, and brooks in forests. Deforestation due to logging is a common threat to the species, so it is important to assess the effects of this disturbance (Johnston et al.2002). Salamander data was collected at Mack Creek and provided by the Environmental Data Initiative (EDI) data portal. The only two measurements taken from the data are the counts and weights for the Pacific giant salamander. This study presents annual salamander counts at the 2 forest sections of Mack Creek, a table of 2017 salamander counts by channel classification, and other visual figures. Specifically, the observations (abundance and weight) of Pacific giant salamanders at each site are analyzed to determine if there is an association between channel classification (pool, cascades, and side-channel) and forest section (old growth and clear cut).

Data & Methods

Pacific giant salamanders were captured and weighed at the Mack Creek in the H. J. Andrews Experimental Forest and data from 1987 present were provided by EDI data portal (Gregory 2016). 150 m reaches are divided into 3 contiguous 50 m reaches, which are blocked off with nets. Each 50 m sub-reach is then sampled with two-pass electrofishing. For more specific geographic sampling coordinates, see metadata (link to metadata). The abundance and weight were compared at 3 channel classifications for 2 types of forest section by chi-squared test, two sample t-test, Levenes test, one-way Anova and Tukeys test ( = 0.05 throughout). All analyses and figures were prepared using R software version 3.6.1.

Results

Results A

Pacific giant salamander abundance

Salamander count in clear cut (CC) and old growth (OG) sections of Mack Creek from 1993 to 2017. Trends are fairly similar in both sections, with salamander abundance increasing sharply in 2002, decreasing rapidly around the year 2012, and then increasing again beginning in 2015. For all but two years from 1993 to 2014 salamander count in the old growth section was greater than in the clear cut section, with the two exceptions being 1994 and 2012, where salamander count in the clear cut section slightly exceeded salamander observations in the old growth section. Salamander count in the clear cut section was greater than in the old growth section for all three years from 2015 to 2017. Figure 1. Results C

Forest condition impact on Pacific giant salamander location in 2017

Forest condition (clear cut or old growth) did not have a significant effect on salamander location within the channel (cascade, side-channel, pool) ((\(\chi\)2(2)) = 5.5412722), p > 0.05). For salamander observations in the clear cut section n = 368, for observations in the old growth section n = 320. The greatest proportion of salamanders observed was in the cascades (67.12% clear cut, 62.81% old growth), while the smallest proportion of salamanders observed was in the pools (8.42% clear cut, 14.06% old growth). Actual counts for clear cut areas was 247 in cascades, 31 in pools, and 90 in side-channels, and for old growth forest, 201 in cascades, 45 in pools, and 74 in side-channels (Table 1).

Results D

Comparing Pacific giant salamander weight in 2017

Mean salamander weight in clear cut and old growth sections did not differ significantly in 2017 (t(692.79) = -1.67, p > 0.05). Mean salamander weight in the clear cut section was 18.11% greater than in the old growth section, with an actual difference of 1.19 grams. For salamanders observed in the clear cut section mean = 7.78g, n = 368, while for salamanders observed in the old growth section, mean = 6.58g, n = 320.

One-way ANOVA and Tukeys test

Mean salamander weight (g) differed significantly across all three groups (one-way ANOVA with post-hoc Tukeys HSD, F(2, 684) = 4.22, p = 0.015). Post-hoc Tukeys HSD shows a significant difference in mean weight between side-channel and pool salamanders (p < 0.05). Mean weight of salamanders found in pools was 64% greater than that of side-channel salamanders, with an actual difference of 3.61g, where for side-channel salamanders, mean = 5.68g, n = 164, and for pool salamanders, mean = 9.29g, n = 76 (Figure 2). Pool and cascade salamanders, and side-channel and cascade salamanders did not differ significantly in mean weight (p > 0.05). For cascade salamanders, mean = 7.52g, n = 448. Mean weight of pool salamanders was 23% greater than cascade salamanders, while mean weight of cascade salamanders was 32% greater than side channel salamanders. Standard deviations for all groups are fairly high (cascade SD = 9.03, pool SD = 13.62, side-channel SD = 8.27), but can be accounted for due to several outliers in each groups, influencing the data spread (Figure 2).

Summary

  • Abundance of salamanders are similar in clear cut and old growth forest sections of Mack Creek from 1993 to 2017.
  • Forest condition did not have a significant effect on salamander location within the channel classifications
  • Mean salamander weight in clear cut and old growth forest sections did not differ significantly in 2017
