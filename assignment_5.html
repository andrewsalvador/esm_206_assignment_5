Results A

Pacific giant salamander abundance

Salamander count in clear cut (CC) and old growth (OG) sections of Mack Creek from 1993 to 2017. Trends are fairly similar in both sections, with salamander abundance increasing sharply in 2002, decreasing rapidly around the year 2012, and then increasing again beginning in 2015. For all but two years from 1993 to 2014 salamander count in the old growth section was greater than in the clear cut section, with the two exceptions being 1994 and 2012, where salamander count in the clear cut section slightly exceeded salamander observations in the old growth section. Salamander count in the clear cut section was greater than in the old growth section for all three years from 2015 to 2017. Figure 1. Sample salamander counts from years 1993 to 2017 for clear cut (CC) and old growth (OG) sections of Mack Creek. Clear cut section shown in red while old growth section shown in blue. Results C

Forest condition impact on Pacific giant salamander location in 2017

Forest condition (clear cut or old growth) did not have a significant effect on salamander location within the channel (cascade, side-channel, pool) ((\(\chi\)2(2)) = 5.5412722), p > 0.05). For salamander observations in the clear cut section n = 368, for observations in the old growth section n = 320. The greatest proportion of salamanders observed was in the cascades (67.12% clear cut, 62.81% old growth), while the smallest proportion of salamanders observed was in the pools (8.42% clear cut, 14.06% old growth). Actual counts for clear cut areas was 247 in cascades, 31 in pools, and 90 in side-channels, and for old growth forest, 201 in cascades, 45 in pools, and 74 in side-channels (Table 1).

Results D

Comparing Pacific giant salamander weight in 2017

Mean salamander weight in clear cut and old growth sections did not differ significantly in 2017 (t(692.79) = -1.67, p > 0.05). Mean salamander weight in the clear cut section was 18.11% greater than in the old growth section, with an actual difference of 1.19 grams. For salamanders observed in the clear cut section mean = 7.78g, n = 368, while for salamanders observed in the old growth section, mean = 6.58g, n = 320.

One-way ANOVA and Tukey’s test

Mean salamander weight (g) differed significantly across all three groups (one-way ANOVA with post-hoc Tukey’s HSD, F(2, 684) = 4.22, p = 0.015). Post-hoc Tukey’s HSD shows a significant difference in mean weight between side-channel and pool salamanders (p < 0.05). Mean weight of salamanders found in pools was 64% greater than that of side-channel salamanders, with an actual difference of 3.61g, where for side-channel salamanders, mean = 5.68g, n = 164, and for pool salamanders, mean = 9.29g, n = 76 (Figure 2). Pool and cascade salamanders, and side-channel and cascade salamanders did not differ significantly in mean weight (p > 0.05). For cascade salamanders, mean = 7.52g, n = 448. Mean weight of pool salamanders was 23% greater than cascade salamanders, while mean weight of cascade salamanders was 32% greater than side channel salamanders. Standard deviations for all groups are fairly high (cascade SD = 9.03, pool SD = 13.62, side-channel SD = 8.27), but can be accounted for due to several outliers in each groups, influencing the data spread (Figure 2).
